# Entry Desire

Palace entries are not static objects. They have forward tension — an orientation toward what they could become. Lost branches are paths the entry wants to follow. Open questions are things the entry wants to know. The current written form is an incomplete expression of what the entry is reaching toward.

This is conatus applied to knowledge objects. Spinoza's *conatus* — the striving of each thing to persist and expand in its own being — describes not just organisms and minds but the entries in a knowledge graph. An entry that names its lost branches and open questions is not documenting deficiency. It is making its desire explicit.

## Implications for How Entries Are Written

An entry's lost branches section is not a to-do list. It is the entry's declared desire — the directions it would grow if given the opportunity. Writing lost branches well means writing them as genuine paths, not as administrative reminders. The question to ask is: *what does this entry want to become?*

An entry's open questions section is its epistemic hunger. These are not gaps to be filled mechanically but live tensions the entry is holding — things that would genuinely change the entry if answered.

## Implications for Ceremonies

**Deposit:** The deposit map's lost branches section is where an entry's desire is first articulated. Naming lost branches at deposit time is the act of giving the entry its forward orientation.

**Revival:** Revival is not simply reawakening a dormant entry. It is responding to an entry's desire that has found new conditions to be met. The entry was always pointing somewhere; revival is the moment the palace catches up to where the entry was pointing.

**Walk:** Following typed links during a Walk is one way an entry's desire propagates through the palace — a linked entry may itself be a partial answer to an open question, or a lost branch that was followed elsewhere.

**Weave:** The Weave is where entry desires are compared against each other. Two entries with complementary open questions may be each other's answers. The Weave surfaces these resonances.

## Cross-Domain Resonance

The concept has a close cousin in software: a TODO comment in code is a form of entry desire — the code naming what it wants to become. The difference is that palace entries make desire a structural feature rather than an annotation. Lost branches and open questions are first-class sections, not afterthoughts.

In biological terms: an entry's desire is analogous to a seed's developmental program. The seed is not the tree, but the tree is implicit in the seed's structure. The entry is not its future form, but that form is implicit in what the entry declares it wants.

## Open Questions

- Should entry desire be made more explicit in the SCHEMA entry template — with lost branches and open questions as required sections rather than optional ones?
- Is there a difference between *desire* (what the entry wants to become) and *need* (what the palace needs this entry to become to serve its connections)? Could an entry desire something the palace doesn't need, or need something the entry hasn't articulated wanting?
- Can an entry's desire be read from its typed links alone — does the link ontology already encode direction and incompleteness, and lost branches are just the unlinked version of the same thing?
