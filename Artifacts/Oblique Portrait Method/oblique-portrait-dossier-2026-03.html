The Transcript
A complete record of the interrogation. The subject did not know what was being built.
Interviewer
Tell me about a room from your childhood that you remember with unusual clarity — not because something important happened there, but just because of how it felt to be in it.
Subject
My attic bed attached to my bedroom.
Interviewer
An attic bed. So you slept above your own room — in the ceiling of your own life, in a way. What was the quality of the light up there?
Subject
Diffuse and wavy through glass block.
Interviewer
Glass block. So the world outside was present but made strange — recognizable shapes dissolved into something almost liquid.

Completely different direction now. What's something you find genuinely, helplessly funny that you suspect most people around you would not find funny at all?
Subject
When people reveal a fundamental contradiction in their own worldview.
Interviewer
There's a particular pleasure in that — being the only one in the room who sees the seam split open. You're not laughing at them, exactly. It's more like watching a magic trick fail in slow motion.

Different direction entirely. When did you last feel genuinely, physically cold — and what did you do with your body in response?
Subject
This morning. I pretended I didn't feel cold, secretly braced myself — outwardly no change.
Interviewer
Stoic on the outside, quietly armored on the inside. You held the shape of yourself while something else happened underneath.

Tell me about a time you were wrong about a person — not morally wrong, just categorically wrong. You had them filed under the wrong heading entirely.
Subject
An old boss of mine. He seduced me with charm, money, glory and compliments. He was a conniving asshole.
Interviewer
The seduction was the real story — and you probably knew something was off before you let yourself know you knew.

What did you want to be when you were nine years old?
Subject
I never wanted to be anything. I said I wanted to be a musician/composer, but inside I just waited. I was a good musician so I let people think it was my desire, but really it was just a thing I did because it was easy and I enjoyed being good and perhaps praised for something.
Interviewer
So you were already performing a self at nine. The musician wasn't a dream — it was a convenient story that fit the shape others needed you to have.

That word you used — waited — what were you waiting for, do you think? Or was the waiting itself the thing?
Subject
Waiting for desire, waiting to want, waiting for ambition perhaps.
Interviewer
Waiting for desire to arrive like weather. Not forcing it, just leaving the window open.

Sharp left turn. What's something that other people find deeply pleasurable that you have genuinely never understood the appeal of — not morally, not intellectually, just viscerally?
Subject
Musical theatre.
Interviewer
Not even a flicker. Just — nothing.

Tell me about a time you failed at something in a way that nobody ever found out about.
Subject
I failed at being a great musician. Everyone assumes I am a great musician, but I'm really not. I can't play very well. I don't know maybe songs. I can't recognize chords by ear. But because I have found a path of success around music, people assume I am a great musician. But I failed at achieving that.
Interviewer
That's a very particular kind of failure — one where the success is the evidence of the failure. You built a road around the mountain instead of climbing it, and now everyone thinks you've been to the top.

Complete change of register. Describe your relationship to clutter. Physical clutter, in the spaces where you live and work.
Subject
I can't work until it is organized. To me, perhaps to others it would look like clutter.
Interviewer
So there's a real order underneath — it just speaks a private language. The system is legible to you and only you.

When you're in a group of people and the energy goes wrong — the conversation dies, or someone says something that lands badly — what do you do with your body?
Subject
I assume a relaxed posture and observe. I am always comfortable in a group, because I look to figure people out like they were math equations.
Interviewer
People are puzzles you're already solving before they've finished their first sentence. Comfort as a byproduct of analysis — the observation is the participation.

What are you afraid of that you think you probably shouldn't be afraid of?
Subject
Some bugs. Snakes. Shoe stores.
