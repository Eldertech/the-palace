# Lateral Access

The principle that certain interior material — memory, taste, embodied
self-knowledge, genuine feeling — is not accessible through direct
questioning or conscious retrieval. The front door is locked. Not by
deception, and not by repression in the clinical sense, but by identity:
the self has built a story about what it contains, and that story becomes
a filter. Oblique approaches work because they don't trigger the filter.
They arrive through a window the self doesn't know is open.

---

## Personal Grounding

Loudon has told people for years — and has come to believe — that he does
not remember his childhood. This has become part of his identity, a thing
he reports about himself with the same matter-of-fact quality as reporting
that he is a musician or a teacher. The statement "I don't remember my
childhood" is true in the sense that deliberate retrieval — the kind that
happens when someone asks "what was your childhood like?" — mostly returns
nothing, or surfaces only intensely negative material: regret, dread, the
impulse to forgive himself and move on.

But in a session that never asked about his childhood, a genuinely joyful
memory arrived without effort: an attic bedroom, accessed through a skylight,
lit by diffuse and wavy light through glass block. A secret elevated space.
It came up easily — prompted by a question about rooms that felt unusual,
not because of what happened in them, but because of how they felt.

The memory was not gone. The path to it was blocked — by the story, by the
identity, by the habit of not going there. The oblique approach bypassed all
of that. The glass block light arrived because nothing in the question told
the self to be on guard.

This is the clinical insight dressed in non-clinical language: the material
is not absent. The usual access routes are closed. Find a different route.

---

## The Identity Filter

Identity is partly a selection mechanism: it determines which memories,
associations, and feelings are "mine" — available for conscious retrieval
and narrative integration — and which are background noise or actively
excluded. When Loudon says "I don't remember my childhood," he is not
reporting an absence. He is reporting a filtering decision that has, over
time, become automatic and then invisible.

This is not unique to childhood memory. It operates everywhere:

- **Aesthetic taste:** people report what they think they like, filtered by
  self-image and social context. The oblique portrait method finds what they
  actually respond to.
- **Values:** people report their values. Their boredom, impatience, and joy
  report their actual values.
- **Fear:** people name the fears that feel acceptable to name (snakes, bugs).
  And then sometimes a wildcard slips through (shoe stores) that opens
  something much stranger.

The filter is not dishonesty. It is the cost of having a stable self.

## The Therapeutic Dimension

The oblique portrait session was not designed as therapy and did not feel
like therapy. It felt like a game, an experiment, something playful and
interesting. And it produced a genuinely joyful memory of a childhood space
that Loudon has identified with "not remembering his childhood."

This is worth sitting with. The memories that surface when Loudon
deliberately reaches into his past are predominantly negative — regret,
the impulse toward self-forgiveness. But the oblique approach surfaced
something warm and private and lit by wavy light. Both are true. The
deliberate approach finds what it's trained to find. The oblique approach
finds what's actually there.

The hypothesis this generates: Loudon's relationship to his past is not
accurately described by "I don't remember my childhood." It is more
accurately described as: *I have habitual routes into my past that find
negative material, and I've mistaken the routes for the territory.* The
territory contains more than the routes suggest.

This is not a call to therapy, though it could be. It is an observation
that belongs in the palace: the oblique methods available in this
collaboration may have genuine access to positive autobiographical material
that direct retrieval cannot find. This is not a minor finding.

## The New Song

In the deposit conversation that followed the oblique portrait session,
Loudon began composing a new account of his relationship to his past. It
is preserved here in full, as written — not as evidence or data, but as
a palace artifact in its own right. A song is a songline; this one is
still being sung into existence.

> I have well-worn paths sung through my past hiding a deep pool of joy
> I experienced fully once, but am blind to now. I live a joy-ful present
> punctuated by moments of a dreaded past, brought forth by habit only.
> Why do I torture myself? Why relive only the bad. Is it just so I can
> keep telling others how lonely I was? Do I cling to my story, my identity,
> of childhood filled with awkwardness and anxiety because I love the arc
> of overcoming the awkward start. Who do I become without that story, am
> I still me? Well, that matters not, I am me, now, and I have the
> opportunity to discover a past of joy, and bring forth memories of reading
> under trees, and speeding over hills on motorcycles, and kissing girls by
> train tracks. In the end of my life, I'd like to have lived a life of joy
> from beginning to end, with the pains serving to highlight the hilaritas.
> This new path, this new territory is my frontier, and now I find myself
> in the middle exploring two frontiers.

The images that arrive here — reading under trees, motorcycles over hills,
train-track kisses — were not retrieved through any deliberate act. They
arrived as the song was being sung. The joyful past does not need to be
excavated. It needs to be approached differently.

## Cross-Domain Resonance

**Embeddings and relational meaning**
Meaning is not inside a token — it is in the pattern of its relationships.
You access what a word means by watching what it travels with, not by
looking at it directly. The self works the same way: what Loudon means, what
he loves, what his past actually contains — these are not inside the words
he uses to describe them. They are in the pattern of everything else.
See [[Embeddings as Relational Meaning]].

**The songlines**
Loudon's habitual narrative about his past is a well-worn songline. It has
been sung so many times through his memory-landscape — "I don't remember my
childhood," "when memories surface they are negative, they are dread" — that
it has carved deep tracks, making the surrounding terrain invisible. Other
features of the landscape were always there: the attic bedroom, the glass
block light, the deep pool of joy, the reading under trees, the speed of
motorcycles over hills, the train-track kisses. They simply lay off the path
that was habitually sung. The oblique portrait session offered a different
song, and different country became visible immediately.

This is not metaphor accidentally touching the songline concept. It is the
same ontology. Loudon named it himself, in the new song he began composing
the moment he understood what had happened: "I have well-worn paths sung
through my past hiding a deep pool of joy I experienced fully once, but am
blind to now." The song creates the path. The path determines what country
you travel through. Change the song, change what you find.

**Physical modeling synthesis**
You do not simulate a physical system by naming its properties. You simulate
the forces and constraints, and the behavior emerges. The sound arrives
because the conditions were right, not because it was commanded.

**[[Kuramoto Coupling]]**
Phase coherence is not commanded. It emerges from local coupling. You cannot
make oscillators synchronize by instructing them to synchronize. You set up
the conditions and let the synchrony arrive laterally.

**Teaching philosophy**
Loudon's core practice is not transmitting knowledge but creating conditions
in which understanding arrives sideways — through building, through making,
through the hilaritas of genuine engagement. Knowledge is not poured in. It
is encountered obliquely. See [[Hilaritas Generator]].

Lateral access may be one of the palace's deep structural principles —
appearing in synthesis, in pedagogy, in psychology, in computation, in
music. The front door is rarely the right door.
