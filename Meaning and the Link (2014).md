# Meaning and the Link (2014)

Loudon's 2014 semantic web paper. Its central thesis: **links are ontologically prior to nodes**. Meaning arises not from symbols themselves but from the sufficient accumulation of associations between them — from the web of relations, not the things being related.

The paper argued this in the technical language of web architecture: RDF predicates (edges, links) are more fundamental than RDF subjects and objects (nodes). The link *is* the meaning. The node is a convenience — an address for a bundle of relations.

The archived PDF lives locally in the palace at `[[Meaning and the Link(2014).pdf]]`.

## Origin

Written in 2014 as a semantic web architecture argument as part of Loudon's MFA coursework at Emerson College. Recognized with Claude in March 2026 — during a Deleuzian dialogue session (H092) — as Deleuzian metaphysics written in the technical language of web architecture. The paper's claim that relations precede relata is exactly Deleuze's claim that difference is primary, identity secondary. It was a philosophical position all along.

## What It Established

**Relations before things.** A word has no meaning alone — only its pattern of associations with other words constitutes its meaning. A node in a network has no identity independent of its edges. This is not just a design preference; it is a claim about the structure of reality.

**Meaning through accumulation.** Sufficient associations between a pattern of symbols and a reality *form* meaning. The threshold matters — sparse connections produce ambiguity; dense, typed connections produce understanding.

**The predicate as first-class citizen.** In standard data models, the link (predicate, verb, relation) is a second-class connector between first-class things. The paper inverted this: the link carries the ontological weight.

## Open Questions

- The paper was written before the transformer architecture made embeddings central to AI. Would the argument look different written today?
- Is there a version of this thesis that extends to time — are *events* also defined by their relational web rather than their intrinsic properties? (Whitehead territory.)
- The paper argued for the semantic web as an infrastructure. The semantic web largely failed to take hold publicly. The palace is a private semantic web — does scale change the ontological claim?
